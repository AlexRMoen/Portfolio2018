your news

Xbox One News App
Microsoft Garage Internship 2017

In Winter 2017, I had the honor of being accepted into Microsoft's Garage Internship program. The Garage is an innovative program that encourages a kind of start-up/maker mindset within the company.

We set about creating an Xbox One app that would deliver personalized newsmedia to TV viewers. I was the sole UX designer with a team of engineers. The project was a success and we shipped in May 2017.

Microsoft had only recently started putting non-gaming apps on their Xbox One game platform, thanks to their UWP application framework. The first one to appear was the MSN Weather app.

We began our internship process with a hackathon. We were assigned one concept: bringing MSN News to the Xbox. As a UX designer, I had to group up with a couple of engineers.

We were given three days to get as far as we could in design and code. I let my team to discuss the broad needs of the design on a whiteboard. After that, we got to work.

I produced a mockup of how the MSN News experience could work on TV. I decided to focus on video content, with text-based content being a poor fit for the TV format. The experience would let you play through a playlist of content, which could be navigated at the user's will.

Meanwhile, my engineers got a simple prototype of this app working on a Windows 10 PC with an Xbox controller connected.

The hackathon was a success, and we recieved very positive feedback. Both myself and my two engineers were assigned this project for the duration of the internship.

My prototype design was fine for a broad concept, but I needed to iterate on the finer details of the UI. I set about designing layouts + interactions on paper - or rather, on a Windows Surface with pen.

Because this was a Microsoft Xbox project, it was a good idea to stick to the established design language of the Xbox One. I studied some of the existing media players on Xbox (top), and recreated their tile-based playlist style. (bottom)

When used as an overlay atop full-screen video, it created an appealing mockup in the style of Xbox.

I modified the tiles to look more "video-like". I also created a "favorites" page, that allows the user to customize different categories of story in tabs at the top of the overlay.

The key to improving the design of the UI is feedback. I systematically collected every piece of feedback I received, and posted it in our office space. Each one of those would be addressed one-by-one by the team, and then discarded or added as an action item.

I also created a visual map of the current site design, so that the team could visualize what it is that they were coding.

One whiteboard served as a space for democratizing design decisions. For every design item for which there was a question, I posted a visualization of it and solicited feedback from within and outside of the team.

We even brought outside playtesters into the building and surveyed them as to how they intuitively felt the design should be. This is how we strengthened the design and made it truly human-centric.

Our app needed a logo in the flat design currently used by Xbox. I explored the current iconography for news, and several symbols emerged: microphones + newspapers.

The final design combined two elemental symbols: a newspaper and a classic antenna TV.

We finished the Xbox One app and brought it to a final showcase in the Microsoft campus. I created a poster that showcased our design process and the features of the app.

The app was well received at the showcase, and less than a month later, the app appeared on the Xbox One Store for the whole world to download.

greenlight arcade

UX Design + Web Development
Internship 2016

In the fall of 2016, I was contracted to design and code an indie game retail website called Greenlight Arcade.

It is not only an e-commerce site selling indie game keys, but it is itself gamiefied functioning like a game itself. This was a great opportunity to build a web experience from the ground up.

This was built on a previous website, also called Greenlight Arcade, but none of the old site content would be carried over. Only the "green sprite" icon would survive into the new version.

This site would allow you earn experience and build your character level, just like an RPG video game. You could also earn "items". All of these would grant you real life discounts on indie games.

The indie games on this site would be given away in "collections". After a few quick sketches I quickly turned to Axure in order to show the manager how these collections could wrap and display each game. His positive feedback kept me on that path.

Within a day, I had come up with the first mockup for this website. It was intended as a "toe in the water", to see how this sat with management.

Because key members of the management were doing business in Asia, I prepared a document that would annotate my design. Without face-to-face feedback, I needed to communicate the details of how it would work.

The clean look was appreciated, but the feedback from management was "too mobile game". They wanted more core gamer "retro game". I could relate, and went back to work.

I delivered a second design, which was slightly more core-focused.

Even as I submitted this mockup, I questioned whether it was "indie game" enough. This time, alongside my mockup annotations, I submitted a document that explores the concept of how "retro" we should go. Management loved it, and told me exactly where on the scale they preferred it.

I submitted a third mockup design. This time, I enclosed a document that showed how it honored classic retro video game aesthetics and colors.

In this design, each collection would feature a primary color (each of which was sourced from classic ZX Spectrum games).

This is how each individual game was featured. I took care not to go too retro computer, and keep some amount of full-bleed images and flat design in order to still feel like a clean, modern website.

There were two important sub-sections to the site: the user panel (showing customer stats) and the shopping cart. I came up with a theory that they should each be represented by "tabs" which display information, and can be clicked on to expand into a videogame-like second menu.

With this third design enthusiastically recieved, I set about coding it. I handled all of the front-end design code. Between HTML, CSS and JavaScript, I wrote over 100,000 lines of code.

I worked closely with fellow engineers who helped build the backend database that would dynamically generate each page of the site.

Restaurant Menu
Freelance 2016

The picturesque mountainscape of Grande Cache, Alberta is home to one of the most delicious sushi restuarants you will ever find. In Summer 2016 I was asked to design their menu.

I took insiration from the artful hand-drawn menus that the Japanese are known for, adding touches of Canadiana to serve their Aurora Borealis-inspired name. Accurate Japanese writing acts as a design element for the mostly English-speaking customers. The circular brush strokes are a popular symbol of Japan, derived from Zen.

a sunny spot

Short Documentary Film
SFU Project 2016

It was a compressed "intersession" version of the Moving Images course, and we had a month to create a short documentary about a real-world subject.

Little did I realize that this time-constrained course would connect me with an adorable older couple who run a Chinese café. I directed the documentary about their story, with the aid of a talented crew of fellow students.

Every documentary begins with a "treatment", a short synopsis of what the film is to be about. In this case, it was created by MacKenzie Ma. She knew of an older couple who ran a Chinese café, famous for their delicious "Xianburgers", and she wanted to document their story.

MacKenzie approached me to join her team. Her and I would work in close collaboration over the next weeks. We were co-directors.

Even documentaries need scripts, in order to plan how the shoot will work. I penned a script that works in three acts: the introduction to the couple, the revelation that they were about the retire, and the satisfacion with which they looked back on their work.

This script helped MacKenzie to write interview questions which would tease out this narrative. She would conduct the interview in Chinese.

Our first day of shooting was before we went to the café. We needed to shoot "b-roll" footage of the surrounding neighbourhood. This would be intercut with the interview footage, rooting the story in Vancouver's unique Main St. neighborhood.

On the main day of shooting, we set up three cameras at different angles, each run by a different crew member. I was doing dual duty as director and boom mic operator, holding a microphone over the interview subject.

I reminded MacKenzie of each question to ask, and she did wonderfully. Our café couple delivered entertaining answers to our questions, and delivered the narrative we'd hoped for. After shooting some more interior b-roll footage of the café and its adorable details, we had a film.

We needed to subtitle the film for our largely English-speaking audience. I had the crew transcribe the couple's responses and translate them into English.

I went over their literal transcription and created my own interpretation in English, checking with MacKenzie to ensure that the meaning was intact. It was okay if some nuances were changed as long as it worked for the intention of the film.

MacKenzie and I edited the film together in Adobe Premiere. I needed her to tell me when certain Chinese sentences began and ended, so the editing would feel right. We chose a music track together and I made sure that the cuts were nicely timed to each section of the song.

I added titles, subtitles, and an ending credits with Adobe AfterEffects. It was also used to perform the "Ken Burns effect" on certain still photographs used in the film.

We screened the film for our teacher and a group of our peers. We could tell from the mood of the room that we had emotionally connected with our audience. We felt proud of our accomplishment, and very happy that we could deliver a retirement gift to the lovely couple who'd been so kind as to star in our film.

kojiro izakaya

Restaurant Menu App UI Design
SFU Project 2016

The course was Interface Design, and I was working with a talented team of students to create an interactive menu for a restaurant setting. Our vision was to place an interactive mobile tablet in front of every customer in the restaurant, replacing the traditional paper menu.

I started prototyping app layouts on paper. It made sense to have a persistent shopping cart docked to the side of the screen that customers could easily add food items to.

I took the concept into digital wireframes using Axure. The goal here is to keep the fidelity low in order to test layout, rather than colors, images, etc.

After some feedback, I opted to change the center of the menu into a vertical scroll. I then created high fidelity mockups in Adobe Illustrator, going for a finished look that honored the aesthetic of a classy Japanese izakaya.

I then took the mockups into Adobe AfterEffects, in order to animate it and show how the app operates. The final result would look identical to a fully-coded app.

ella

App UI Design
SFU 24h Challenge 2016

This was a very quick design process. I had 24 hours to design an app that would help users find volunteering opportunities.

The goal was to help users into the app as effortlessly as possible. To that end, you are able to log in with popular social media services.

Inspired by Apple Music's onboarding process, the user is offered a constallation of types of volunteer opportunities. The app will therefore know how to better suggest interesting opportunities to the user.

Upon entering the app, the user is suggested volunteering opportunities. They can swipe left and right between different jobs, or choose type filters at the top of the screen.

The user is able to favorite the volunteer opportunities that they are most interested in, so they they can return to them later.

playboy

Website UI Design
SFU Project 2015

Yes, that Playboy. In 2015, the magazine announced that it was removing adult pictures from the magazine.

Our team in Russell Taylor's Interactive Objects & Environments decided to envision what a Playboy service would look like if it were focused on great articles.

Our team wrote down a list of Playboy's values. We determined that there was a respected element to the magazine which had been largely forgotten since the internet. They used to feature articles written by respected authors in between the adult content.

We wanted to bring that back in a modern digital context.

The mobile broadcast concept wasn't gaining much traction with our panel of critics, so we pivoted to a website that focused on provocative authors.

I iterated on a series of designs on paper. I came to the idea of a timeline "tree" formation. Each new story would appear on the tree as a different "leaf" along the timeline.

I proceeded to final mockups of the timeline tree concept in Adobe Illustrator. Each topic of discussion would be given a different color, causing the tree to display a rainbow of colors as it developed.

We shot a pitch video, showing how the author can contribute to the site, and how the user can read them and re-blog them like a social media site.

Other members of the team animated my mockups to be added to the pitch video. Meanwhile, I created and edited the video. I used imagery of classic Playboy history, alongside the perhaps-surprising revelation of how many respected authors have contributed to the magazine in the past.

We completed our mockup of how this new Playboy author-focused site would work, and detailed its social media-style features in our pitch video.

The pitch was well received by a panel of critics.

enterprise

Mobile App UI Design
SFU Project 2015

For Professor Russell Taylor's Interactive Objects & Environments class, we were assigned a design exercise where we create a project for Enterprise Rent-A-Car.

In an environment of car-sharing services like Uber and Lyft, we envisioned an app which would bring vehicles to the user's location.

Our team wrapped our heads around the entire process of renting a car. We wrote down every point of contact between company and customer on sticky notes, in order to define all the factors that affect them.

We proposed a service that went beyond Enterprise's current "We'll pick you up" concept. In today's app economy, the vehicle should come to you.

I mocked up slides that showed a broad interface concept that would help users to select a vehicle for delivery.

Our feedback was to focus this service on a mobile app.

I started thinking about the limited screen space of mobile, and how a user could swipe left or right to navigate between car choices. Within 10 minutes, I mocked up this concept to communicate to my team.

Our team worked together and arrived at a final app design. I created the mockups in Adobe Illustrator.

This is the front page of the app, showing the user's location. The app will help them to select vehicles that are in the area.

The buttons at the bottom of the screen expand into "drawers".

Each drawer allows you to select the date and time of your travel.

Once you've selected your travel time, the app displays a full screen image of a car in your location. You can swipe left and right to choose others.

There is also a filter button, which displays a list of toggles that filter for specific vehicle types.

Once a vehicle has been selected, you are able to select specific options for that vehicle.

Enter your payment method, and confirm your order.

Once a vehicle has been ordered, the order appears on the front page of the app. You are able to quickly return to it when you return to the app.

We shot a quick pitch video. I prepared text motion graphics, while a teammate edited the video.

We presented it to our class, and recieved positive feedback.
